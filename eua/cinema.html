The Joker's Curse


The story goes that all actors who live in the character's skin in theaters end up going crazy, even for a period of time. The first to play Joker in a live action was Cesar Romero in the 1960s on a TV series called "Batman." The actor was the most rejected joker by comic book audiences, claiming that he lacked the character's homicidal essence and just turned the villain into a clown. The actor even declared that there was a dissociation between him and the character he could not cross or capture, which ended up leaving him confused, insecure and with severe headaches.

More than twenty years later, in 1989, Jack Nicholson ("The Shining", "Chinatown") lived the character in the film directed by Tim Burton, also called "Batman". At first, the actor loved the creative freedom of the role and was also living in the villain's skin 24 hours a day, so much so that when the movie came out he was praised by fans for balancing the psychopathic and humorous characteristics of the character. But the role began to interfere with your personal, social and professional life. He complained of insomnia and migraines that only passed when the recordings were finished.

Then came the heaviest case of all. Heath Ledger ("10 Things I Hate About You", "The Brokeback Mountain Secret") played the most popular wildcard in movie theaters in the movie "Batman: The Dark Knight," directed by Christopher Nolan. When the role was offered to Ledger, he even scorned the character. At the same time, the actor was going through personal and family problems, which certainly intensified the problems that Joker's experience had presented before: insomnia, anxiety, migraines and depression. A documentary about the actor even gained access to diaries showing how he had been affected by the character, living in it 24 hours a day. Ledger died of an accidental overdose of prescription drugs, doctors said, shortly after the movie was over, leaving his work in "The Imaginary World of Dr. Parnassus" unfinished. Jack Nicholson upon learning that Ledger had died said, "Well, I warned you!" The actor received a posthumous Oscar for best lead actor for his performance as Joker. Even Mark Hammil, known for playing Luke Skywalker in the "Star Wars" saga movies, who has been voicing Joker for the past 20 years in a series of animations, has even complained about the role. Even without directly giving life to the character, Hammil has often referred to the Joker as "an animal" and has also described bouts of anxiety and insomnia.
